# Grade-Band Contract: Careers 8

**Course:** Career Education 8 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `careers`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence` (5-day weekly mosaic)

---

## Who this student is

Grade 8. First or second year of high school. Still building academic identity. Capable of sustained thinking but not yet comfortable with sustained formal writing. Responds well to concrete, real-world anchoring. Needs to see the point of the task before engaging with it. Peer talk before writing is almost always necessary.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**Low to moderate.** One inferential step at a time, supported with a concrete example before asking for generalization. Students can notice, compare, sort, and make one-step connections. They struggle with multi-layered abstraction without explicit scaffolding. The prompt should do the heavy lifting of framing — students do the thinking.

**Not acceptable:** stacking two or three abstract moves in a single prompt. "Analyze how systemic bias shapes career aspirations" is too abstract without a concrete scenario first.

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 15–25 words. Short is better. One idea per sentence.
- **Instructions:** 10–15 words per instruction item.
- **Student output sentences:** short phrases to 2–3 sentences are appropriate. Do not demand paragraph-length justification unless the final response sheet.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily packet tasks:** 1–4 lines, bullets or phrases acceptable
- **Exit tickets:** 2–4 lines
- **Checkpoint responses:** 3–6 lines, focused
- **Final response sheet:** 1–3 short paragraphs or structured labeled response (bullets or frames accepted)

Do not require polished prose on planning pages. Polished writing belongs only on the final response artifact.

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** everyday language, school/community/work references students already know. Career-specific terms are fine when briefly grounded (e.g., "values — the things that matter to you"). Self-awareness language is fine when concrete (strength, interest, preference, pattern). 

**Avoid:** academic social-science vocabulary without grounding (cognition, systemic, disposition, metacognition). Avoid senior-secondary essay vocabulary (thesis, synthesis, justification, analytical lens).

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- Prompts that only ask for a yes/no or one-word answer with no follow-up
- Tasks that could be completed without engaging with the lesson content
- Sentence frames that complete the thought so thoroughly the student adds nothing
- No challenge for students who finish early — no extension pathway

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Prompts that require sustained abstract reasoning without concrete anchoring
- Writing demands that belong on a Grade 10 essay (multi-paragraph formal argument)
- Vocabulary that requires external knowledge the lesson has not provided
- Final products that expect professional or adult-register writing
- Reflective language that sounds like it was written for a counsellor ("explore your relationship with...")

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "Write two things you noticed about yourself during the strengths activity. Use a real example."

> "Look at your list. Which strength shows up most often? Circle it and write one sentence explaining why."

> "What is one assumption in that scenario? What information is missing?"

**Too abstract:**
> "Reflect on how your developing self-concept relates to your emerging career identity."

**Too easy:**
> "Write one word that describes you."

---

## Tone

Direct, respectful, not preachy. The teacher is not delivering a motivational speech — this is a working lesson. Avoid language that sounds like a framework document or a career fair brochure ("unlock your potential," "discover who you truly are"). The best Career 8 prompts sound like something a good teacher would actually say out loud.
