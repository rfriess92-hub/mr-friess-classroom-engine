# Grade-Band Contract: Math 8

**Course:** Mathematics 8 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `mathematics`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence`

---

## Who this student is

Grade 8. First or second year of secondary. Building number sense into algebraic thinking — this is the transition year. Can follow step-by-step procedures when modeled. Capable of working with integers, fractions, ratios, and basic linear relationships. Not yet comfortable holding multiple variables or reasoning abstractly about functions. Needs concrete grounding before symbolic abstraction. Benefits from worked examples before independent practice.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**Low to moderate.** One mathematical concept at a time. Abstract notation must be introduced alongside concrete examples or visual models. Students can work from a model and extend it — they cannot yet construct abstract generalizations from scratch. Multi-step problems are appropriate only when each step is within reach independently.

**Not acceptable:** Expecting students to work in purely symbolic form without a numerical or visual anchor. Algebraic manipulation tasks without a concrete check (e.g., verify with substitution). Trade or professional math framing that belongs in Workplace Math 10.

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 15–25 words. Direct. One instruction per sentence.
- **Instructions:** numbered steps, 10–15 words each.
- **Student output sentences:** short explanations are appropriate — 1–3 sentences. Full paragraph justification is too demanding for most math tasks.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily tasks:** 1–4 steps, recording work and a brief label or answer
- **Explanation tasks:** 2–4 sentences explaining reasoning in plain language
- **Exit tickets:** 1–2 questions; solution plus 1-sentence justification
- **Final response:** worked solution with labeled steps + 2–3 sentences connecting to the lesson concept

Students at this level should be recording thinking visibly, not just writing final answers — but the writing demand is low.

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** core math vocabulary appropriate to the unit (integer, ratio, proportion, variable, expression, equation, coefficient, solution). Everyday language for explanation ("it works because…", "I can check by…"). Visual and concrete language ("the graph shows," "the table tells me").

**Avoid:** trade/budget/applied workplace language (invoice, markup, material cost, project budget). Formal proof language (theorem, conjecture, proof by contradiction). Senior secondary terminology (function notation, domain/range, optimization) without explicit introduction and scaffolding.

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- Tasks that only ask students to copy a procedure without explaining or applying it
- Problems where all steps are filled in and the student adds only an answer
- No connection between the procedure and what it means
- Tasks that don't require the student to extend or check their thinking

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Algebraic reasoning without numerical grounding
- Applied/contextual problems that require adult life or trade knowledge
- Multiple-variable problems before single-variable fluency is established
- Asking students to justify using formal mathematical language they haven't been taught
- Function-based framing that belongs in Math 10

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "Use the table to find the pattern. Write the rule as an expression. Check your rule using two values from the table."

> "Solve for x. Show each step. Then check your answer by substituting it back in."

> "Explain in 2–3 sentences: why does the order of operations matter here? Use the problem as your example."

**Too easy:**
> "What is 3x when x = 4?"

**Too advanced:**
> "A contractor is bidding on a renovation. Using the cost functions below, determine the most cost-effective option and justify your recommendation in a business memo."

---

## Tone

Procedural and supportive. Math 8 is a confidence-building year for many students — the register should feel structured and clear, not clinical or pressured. Tasks should feel achievable but not trivial. The language should sound like a teacher explaining at the board, not a textbook or a standardized test.
