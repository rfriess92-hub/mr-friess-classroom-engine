# Grade-Band Contract: Workplace Math 10

**Course:** Workplace Mathematics 10 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `mathematics`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence`

---

## Who this student is

Grade 10. Pursuing the applied math pathway. Has made a deliberate choice toward practical, context-driven mathematics. Not on the pre-calculus track — this distinction matters for framing. Capable of multi-step applied reasoning when the context is real and the purpose is clear. Can work with measurement, proportion, budgets, schedules, and spatial problems. Reads a problem scenario before extracting numbers. Responds poorly to abstraction for its own sake but engages well when the "why this matters" is embedded in the task.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**Moderate, always applied.** Abstract mathematical relationships must be embedded in a scenario that a student can picture. Multi-step reasoning is appropriate — in fact, expected — when each step connects to the real-world context. Students can make decisions and justify them (e.g., "this option is better because…") when given adequate scenario detail. Can work from diagrams, tables, invoices, floor plans, and schedules.

**Not acceptable:** Decontextualized algebraic manipulation — that belongs in a different math pathway. Tasks that require pre-calculus reasoning or formal proof. Juvenile framing that treats the student as a Grade 8 learner (simple one-step problems, no real stakes, no adult scenario).

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 20–35 words. Can embed scenario context and math task together.
- **Instructions:** can be multi-step if the steps follow logically from the scenario.
- **Student output sentences:** full sentences expected for justification; 2–4 sentences for explanation tasks. Calculation work should be shown with labeled steps.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily tasks:** worked calculation with labeled steps + 1–3 sentence explanation of decision or result
- **Scenario-based problems:** 3–6 steps of visible work + a 2–3 sentence recommendation or finding
- **Exit tickets:** one applied problem solved with brief written rationale
- **Final response:** complete problem solution with labeled steps and a written summary that addresses the scenario question (not just "the answer is X")

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** applied workplace vocabulary appropriate to the scenario (invoice, estimate, scale, proportion, unit rate, markup, overhead, net/gross, budget, tolerance, material cost). Measurement language (imperial and metric). Decision language ("the best option is… because…", "this exceeds the budget by…"). Spatial language (perimeter, area, volume, scale factor).

**Avoid:** formal algebraic proof language. Pre-calculus terminology (function notation, limits, transformations in the abstract sense). Academic register that doesn't belong in an applied course. Vocabulary that treats this as a remedial track — the register should feel professional, not simplified.

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- One-step problems with no real scenario
- Problems that require no decisions — just calculation
- Tasks that don't ask the student to interpret or communicate a result
- Scenarios so simplified they don't resemble actual workplace contexts
- Language that sounds like Grade 8 math (no context, no stakes)

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Formal algebraic reasoning without applied grounding
- Problems requiring calculus-adjacent thinking
- Scenarios requiring professional expertise not provided in the task
- Academic essay-style written justification
- Multiple simultaneous variables without scaffolded setup

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "The client wants 48 square metres of flooring. Tiles come in boxes of 1.2 m². Each box costs $34.50. How many boxes do you need, and what is the total material cost? Show your work."

> "You have a budget of $1,200 for materials. Using the price list, choose three materials and show whether your selection fits the budget. Explain one trade-off you made."

> "The scale on the blueprint is 1:50. Measure the room on the diagram and calculate the actual dimensions. Then find the perimeter."

**Too easy (Grade 8 level):**
> "A store sells apples for $2 each. How much do 6 apples cost?"

**Too advanced (academic math):**
> "Derive the equation of the linear function that models the cost relationship and determine the domain for which the model is valid."

---

## Tone

Applied and respectful. This course has real-world stakes — students should feel that the math they are doing connects to work they might actually do. The register should feel like a competent adult explaining a practical problem, not a textbook author or a career counsellor. Do not frame Workplace Math as a consolation course or a remedial alternative. The problems should be genuinely interesting and multi-step — this is not easier math, it is different math.
