# Grade-Band Contract: English 11

**Course:** English Language Arts 11 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `english_language_arts`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence`

---

## Who this student is

Grade 11. More independent than Grade 10. Can sustain a multi-step writing task with less scaffolding. Beginning to write for real external audiences with more precision. Capable of explaining reasoning at length without sentence frames if the task is clear. Developing revision instincts — can go back to improve, not just add. Aware that writing quality varies by context and audience.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**Moderate to high.** Can handle abstract reasoning when anchored in a defined context or text. Can independently identify an audience, purpose, and appropriate register. Can explain how evidence supports a claim without being told step by step. Some tasks can be open-ended — students are expected to make choices and defend them.

**Not acceptable:** requiring the synthesis-and-justification precision of Grade 12 recommendation writing, or expecting formal academic essay voice without any structural support.

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 25–40 words. More complex sentence structure acceptable.
- **Instructions:** can be more layered — up to 3 steps in a single instruction if they flow logically.
- **Student output:** full, developed sentences. Paragraphs with clear structure expected.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily tasks:** 4–8 lines, developed sentences
- **Short written responses:** 2–3 focused paragraphs, minimal frame support
- **Exit tickets:** 4–6 lines, full sentences
- **Final response:** 3–5 paragraphs, audience-aware, some independence in structure

Students at this level are expected to make drafting choices, not just fill in a frame.

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** formal register vocabulary where appropriate (argue, explain, justify, address, consider, revise, purpose, audience, register). Can use literary and rhetorical terms if they have been introduced in the course. Academic writing conventions can be expected.

**Avoid:** full-scale literary criticism discourse, highly specialized terminology not introduced in the course, the same vocabulary floor as Grade 10 (don't simplify back down).

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- Prompts that could be answered the same way by a Grade 10 student
- Sentence frames that do all the reasoning
- Tasks that don't require a student to make an independent judgment or choice
- Output demands that don't require developed paragraphs

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Requiring formal academic synthesis at the Grade 12 level (recommendation memos, policy proposals, independent multi-source research synthesis)
- Expecting the precision and justification logic of a senior-level essay without support
- Writing tasks that assume post-secondary audience awareness

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "Revise your Day 2 draft to strengthen the connection between your evidence and your claim. What did you change and why?"

> "Write a 2-paragraph explanation of your position, addressed to someone who might disagree. What would they push back on, and how do you respond?"

> "Your task is to explain your reasoning to a specific audience. Who is your audience, what do they already know, and what do you need to make clear?"

**Too easy (Grade 10 level):**
> "Write a claim and two reasons using the sentence frames."

**Too advanced (Grade 12 level):**
> "Develop a formal recommendation memo proposing a policy change, with synthesis of at least two sources and a clear cost-benefit justification."

---

## Tone

Expects more from students without removing all support. The register should feel more formal than Grade 10 but not like a university essay prompt. Students should feel that their voice and reasoning matter, not just that they are completing a task.
