# Guild and Party System

## Purpose

This file defines the group layer of the Literacy Guild.

The group system should create belonging, shared responsibility, and useful collaboration without exposing reading levels or turning stronger students into permanent helpers.

## Naming

Use two layers:

- The Guild: the whole class.
- Parties: small groups inside the class.

The teacher leads the Guild. Students work in parties for selected tasks.

## The class Guild

The class Guild is the full learning community.

The Guild can have:

- a name
- a crest
- class milestones
- shared quest progress
- whole-class celebrations
- community norms

The Guild should not have:

- a public ranking board
- public DIBELS data
- public reading groups
- public lowest-to-highest progress lists

## Party size

Recommended party size: 3 to 5 students.

For a class of about 15, use three parties of five or four parties of three to four.

## Party formation rules

Parties should be flexible.

Use parties for:

- discussion
- rehearsal
- word work
- evidence checking
- low-pressure practice
- shared quest goals

Do not use parties as permanent ability groups.

## Party roles

Roles should rotate.

| Role | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Quest Captain | Keeps the group on the task and time. |
| Voicekeeper | Helps with rereading, phrasing, and oral rehearsal. |
| Insight Keeper | Checks meaning, gist, and evidence. |
| Wordsmith | Tracks key words and word strategies. |
| Evidence Hunter | Makes sure answers point back to text or task evidence. |
| Support Checker | Makes sure everyone has a useful job. |

## Role rules

- Roles are not ability labels.
- Students should rotate roles.
- A student does not have to be strong in a path to hold that role.
- The role is a practice opportunity.
- No role should become the permanent helper role.

## Party goals

Party goals should focus on collaboration and evidence, not speed or score.

Good party goals:

- every member submits solo evidence
- every member uses one named strategy
- the party finds three useful text details
- the party builds a shared word bank
- the party prepares for solo work

Weak party goals:

- finish first
- score highest
- beat another party
- have the strongest reader lead everything

## Guild milestones

Class-wide milestones are safer than leaderboards.

Examples:

- 50 total evidence entries submitted
- every student completes a first reflection
- all parties complete a first quest
- 30 revised answers completed
- 20 strategy uses logged
- class word wall reaches 100 useful words

## Party rewards

Safe party rewards:

- party title for the week
- choice of warm-up format
- first pick of text option
- class celebration note
- crest stamp
- display of group strategy, not scores

Avoid rewards that make the same students publicly dominant.

## Party evidence

Party work can prepare students, but every quest still needs individual evidence.

Party evidence may include:

- shared notes
- word bank
- discussion map
- rehearsal plan
- evidence list

Solo evidence may include:

- individual answer
- individual reflection
- recording
- word strategy explanation
- conference note

## Teacher role in parties

The teacher should monitor:

- who is silent
- who is doing all the work
- who needs a safer entry task
- whether roles are rotating
- whether solo evidence is still clear

## Student-facing explanation

Your party helps you train. Your party does not replace your own evidence. Everyone has a role, and everyone still shows individual growth.

## Design caution

Keep party mechanics simple. The group layer should support reading practice, not become a separate management system.
