# Literacy Guild Campaign Story Spine

## Purpose

This document defines the story spine for the Literacy Guild.

The story should make reading practice feel connected and worth doing. It should not bury the reading work or turn instruction into pretend play.

Reading comes first.

Story gives the work a reason.

## Core premise

There is an Archive full of records, messages, field notes, reports, warnings, maps, and fragments.

The Archive is not broken in a dramatic way. It is damaged in a reading way:

- some records are hard to read aloud clearly
- some words are difficult to unlock
- some passages are hard to understand at first
- some evidence is easy to miss
- some meaning only becomes clear after rereading

The class becomes a reading guild trained to recover, repair, and understand these records.

The guild does not guess.

The guild inspects, reads, rereads, breaks apart, checks, proves, and explains.

## Student role

Students are not heroes because they read fast.

Students are guild members because they practise.

Their character is a way to track how they practise, what strategies they try, and what gets stronger.

A character does not show a reading level.

A character shows training.

## Story tone

Use a calm, slightly mysterious tone.

The story should feel like students are entering a reading mission, not a fantasy costume party.

Use words like:

- archive
- record
- signal
- lock
- gate
- trail
- repair
- recover
- evidence
- proof
- training
- support kit

Avoid overusing fantasy words.

Avoid monsters, battles, spells, magic schools, official role-playing game language, or anything that makes the reading feel like an afterthought.

## Hard science-of-reading rule

Story hooks must not ask students to guess unknown printed words from context.

Do not use hooks like:

- A word is smudged. Guess what would fit.
- The record is missing a word. Use the sentence to guess it.
- Look at the first letter and think of a word.

Use hooks like:

- A word is locked. Take it apart and show how it works.
- A record is hard to read clearly. Practise until the meaning comes through.
- A passage will not open until you can show what it says and where the text proves it.

The story must push students toward decoding, morphology, fluency, vocabulary, syntax, comprehension, and evidence.

## Campaign structure

### Arc 1: Enter the Guild

Story purpose:

Students learn that the guild is a training system for reading.

They learn that help is normal, progress is private, and the game layer is there to track practice.

Reading purpose:

Students learn the class routines:

- try a reading move
- use a stuck list
- use a power-up support
- record evidence
- get feedback
- try again

Core line:

`This class is about getting better at reading. The guild is how we keep track of the work.`

### Arc 2: Voice Trial

Story purpose:

The Archive contains records that can only be understood when they are read clearly.

Students train to make the words sound controlled enough for meaning to come through.

Reading purpose:

Voice Trial builds fluency through:

- teacher model reading
- echo reading
- phrase practice
- punctuation pauses
- repeated reading
- error repair
- private rehearsal before sharing

Good story hook:

`This record is not ready for the Archive until someone can read it clearly enough for the meaning to come through.`

Avoid:

- speed contests
- public ranking
- surprise public reading
- making fluency about performance instead of control

### Arc 3: Word Vault

Story purpose:

The Archive has locked difficult words.

The lock opens when students inspect the word and show how it works.

Reading purpose:

Word Vault builds vocabulary and word knowledge through:

- accurate reading of the word
- syllables when useful
- spelling patterns when useful
- base words
- prefixes and suffixes
- known morphemes
- pronunciation
- student-friendly meaning
- checking meaning in the sentence
- accurate word use

Core line:

`This word is not a mystery to guess. It is a lock to take apart.`

Good story hook:

`The Archive marked this word because it slows readers down. Look closely, break it apart, read it, and show how it works in the sentence.`

Avoid:

- context guessing
- dictionary copying with no understanding
- random hard-word lists
- treating vocabulary as memorization only

### Arc 4: Insight Gate

Story purpose:

A gate in the Archive opens only when students can show what the text says and where the text proves it.

Reading purpose:

Insight Gate builds comprehension through:

- gist statements
- text evidence
- inference with proof
- rereading when meaning breaks
- sentence-level explanation
- oral rehearsal before writing

Good story hook:

`The Gate does not open for guesses. It opens when you can show what the text says and where the text proves it.`

Avoid:

- unsupported inference
- “I just know” answers
- answers copied from the text with no explanation

### Arc 5: Integrated Campaign

Story purpose:

Students work through an Archive case that requires Voice, Wordcraft, and Insight together.

Reading purpose:

Students combine skills:

- read a short passage accurately and clearly
- inspect difficult words
- explain the gist
- find evidence
- repair confusion
- complete a final case response

Good story hook:

`This case cannot be solved with one path only. Read it clearly, unlock the words that slow you down, and prove what the record means.`

Avoid:

- making the campaign a long worksheet packet
- overloading students with plot before they understand the text
- using mystery as an excuse for guessing

### Arc 6: Final Guild Portfolio

Story purpose:

Students show what they recovered and how their reading changed.

Reading purpose:

Students gather evidence of growth:

- a Voice example
- a Wordcraft example
- an Insight example
- a reflection on supports that helped
- a next-step goal

Good story hook:

`The Archive keeps records of what was recovered. Your portfolio shows what you practised, what helped, and what got stronger.`

Avoid:

- ranking characters publicly
- turning the portfolio into a grade label
- using DIBELS or reading level as character worth

## Power-ups in the story

Power-ups are support tools the character can use during a quest.

They are not strategies.

They are not prizes.

They make help visible and usable.

Examples of story-safe power-up language:

- `Use one Echo Charge to hear a line before you read it back.`
- `Use one Reset Shield to stop, breathe, and reread the part that got messy.`
- `Use one Lockpick Clue to ask about one word part.`
- `Use one Evidence Marker to check whether your proof actually matches your answer.`

Power-ups should never let students skip the reading or get the answer for free.

## Strategies in the story

Strategies are plain reading moves.

Students can use them any time.

Story language should not make students think they need permission to use a reading strategy.

Use:

`If you get stuck, use the stuck list first. If you still need help, use a power-up or ask me.`

## Repeated campaign lines

Use these lines across packets and lessons:

- `Reading comes first.`
- `Your progress is private.`
- `Use the support. Show the evidence.`
- `Do not guess. Inspect.`
- `Read it again. Something usually changes.`
- `The text has to prove it.`
- `Your character tracks training, not reading level.`

## Story hook test

Before using a hook, ask:

1. Does this create a real reading task?
2. Does this avoid guessing from context?
3. Does this make students inspect the print, the word parts, the sentence, or the evidence?
4. Does this keep basic help available?
5. Would this sound okay if Mr. Friess said it out loud?

If not, rewrite it.

## Best next build

Do not build another generic packet next.

Build one complete playable lesson:

`Voice Trial Lesson 1 — The First Read`

That lesson should include:

- an original short Archive record
- a teacher model script
- student reading routine
- power-up use point
- stuck-list use point
- student evidence page
- teacher table prompts
- likely responses
- what counts as enough evidence
- recovery moves
- exit check
