# Science-of-Reading Story Rules

## Purpose

Use this page before writing story hooks, quest text, power-ups, teacher guides, or student packets.

The Literacy Guild can have story and plot, but the story must not pull instruction back into weak reading habits.

The rule is simple:

**The story can make the work feel interesting. It cannot replace good reading instruction.**

## Hard rule

Do not ask students to guess unknown printed words from context, pictures, first letters, or what would sound right.

That means avoiding prompts like:

- What word would make sense here?
- Look at the picture and guess.
- Use the sentence to guess the word.
- Skip it and guess from the rest.
- What word starts with that letter and would fit?

These prompts can train students to work around the printed word instead of reading it.

## Better rule

Students inspect the word first.

Then they check meaning in the sentence.

Use this order:

1. Look closely at the word.
2. Read the sounds or known spelling patterns.
3. Break the word into parts if needed.
4. Look for a base word, prefix, suffix, or known morpheme.
5. Read the word again.
6. Check whether the meaning fits the sentence.
7. Use the word in speech or writing.

Short student version:

**Look at the word. Break it apart. Read it. Check it. Use it.**

## Core student line

Use this for Word Vault:

**This word is not a mystery to guess. It is a lock to take apart.**

## Story design rule

Story hooks should create a reason to inspect, decode, reread, explain, or prove.

They should not create a reason to guess.

Good story frame:

> The Archive has locked a difficult word. Take the word apart and show how it works.

Weak story frame:

> The Archive smudged a word. Guess what word would fit.

## Word Vault rule

Word Vault is not context guessing.

Word Vault should focus on:

- accurate reading of the word
- spelling patterns
- syllables when useful
- base words
- prefixes and suffixes
- roots or known word parts when appropriate
- pronunciation
- student-friendly meaning
- how the word works in the sentence
- using the word accurately

Context is used to confirm meaning, not to identify the printed word by guessing.

## Teacher prompt pattern

Use prompts like these:

- Look at the whole word.
- What part do you recognize?
- Can you find a base word?
- Is there a prefix or suffix?
- Read the first part. Now the next part.
- Say the word again.
- What does that part mean?
- Now check the sentence. Does that meaning fit?
- Show me where the sentence supports that meaning.
- Use the word in your own sentence.

Avoid prompts like these:

- Guess from the sentence.
- What would make sense?
- Just skip it and come back with a guess.
- Look at the first letter and think of a word.
- Use the picture to figure it out.

## Voice Trial rule

Voice work should support accurate and fluent reading.

Story hooks should give students a reason to reread, smooth out phrasing, pause at punctuation, repair errors, and build confidence.

Good story frame:

> This record can be understood only if it is read clearly. Practise the line until the meaning comes through.

Voice work can include:

- teacher model reading
- echo reading
- choral reading
- partner reading
- repeated reading
- phrase marking
- punctuation pauses
- error repair
- short private rehearsal before public sharing

Do not turn Voice into speed competition.

## Insight Gate rule

Insight work should focus on comprehension, evidence, and repair.

Story hooks should give students a reason to find gist, prove an answer, make a supported inference, or reread when meaning breaks.

Good story frame:

> The Gate opens only when you can show what the text says and where the text proves it.

Insight work can include:

- gist statements
- text evidence
- inference with proof
- rereading for repair
- sentence-level explanation
- oral rehearsal before writing

Do not reward unsupported guessing.

## Power-up rule

Power-ups are supports, not shortcuts around reading.

A power-up can let a student:

- hear a model
- reread with a partner
- take a reset
- ask for one clue about a word part
- use a stuck-list move
- check evidence with the teacher

A power-up should not say:

- guess the word
- skip the reading
- avoid the evidence
- get the answer for free

## Character rule

Character supports should shape how students get help, not whether they get help.

Basic reading support is always available.

No student should be denied decoding support, vocabulary support, or comprehension support because of character choice, level, or power-up count.

## Story tone

Keep story language clear and age-respectful.

Use:

- record
- archive
- gate
- lock
- signal
- clue
- repair
- recover
- prove
- train

Avoid overdoing fantasy language.

The story should feel like a reading mission, not a costume placed over worksheets.

## Final test for any story hook

Before using a story hook, ask:

1. Does this make students read more carefully?
2. Does this make students inspect the printed word?
3. Does this make students use evidence?
4. Does this avoid guessing from context?
5. Does this keep help available without shame?

If the answer is no, rewrite the story hook.
