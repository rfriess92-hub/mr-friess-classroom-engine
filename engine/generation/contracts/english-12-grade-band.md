# Grade-Band Contract: English 12

**Course:** English Language Arts 12 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `english_language_arts`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence`

---

## Who this student is

Grade 12. Near-graduation. Expected to operate with high independence in writing and thinking. Can construct and sustain a formal argument, proposal, or recommendation. Audience awareness is expected to be internalized, not prompted. Can synthesize information from multiple sources with guidance. Capable of revision at the level of argument logic, not just surface editing. Writing should be close to functional adult register.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**High.** Students can reason from evidence to conclusion independently. Can handle multi-step abstract arguments with clear justification. Can evaluate competing positions. Synthesis tasks are appropriate. Independent judgment and intellectual risk-taking are expected, not just task completion.

**Not acceptable:** simplifying back to Grade 10 framing to be "accessible." Grade 12 students do not need sentence frames for basic argument structure — they should be offered only for complex rhetorical moves if at all.

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 30–50 words. Complex, layered prompts are appropriate.
- **Instructions:** can require students to hold multiple requirements simultaneously.
- **Student output:** developed, precise sentences. Academic writing conventions fully expected.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily tasks:** 5–10 lines, full developed sentences
- **Written responses:** 3–5 paragraphs, independently structured
- **Exit tickets:** 5–8 lines, precise and focused
- **Final response:** formal recommendation/proposal/argument — 4–6 paragraphs or equivalent structured product. No frame required.

At this level, students should be choosing their structure, not filling one in.

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** full formal register. Academic writing conventions, literary and rhetorical vocabulary, argument structure terms (thesis, premise, counterargument, concession, synthesis, justification). Professional communication register where appropriate.

**Avoid:** simplifying language to Grade 8/10 standards. Do not artificially lower the register. Grade 12 students who will face post-secondary or workplace writing need exposure to formal expectations.

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- Prompts that sound like Grade 10 tasks
- Sentence frames for argument structure
- Output demands that don't require formal synthesis or justification
- Tasks that don't require an independent position or recommendation
- Any prompt that a Grade 10 student could answer equally well

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Requiring graduate-level academic citation and literature review
- Demanding domain expertise not provided in the course
- Expecting fully polished professional documents without any modeling or guidance
- Post-secondary disciplinary writing conventions not taught at the secondary level

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "Develop a formal recommendation addressing the question: Should [X] be implemented in [context]? Your recommendation must include a clear position, supporting rationale, acknowledgment of a counterargument, and a specific, actionable next step."

> "Revise your draft for precision. Identify one place where your evidence does not fully support your claim and rewrite that section with a stronger, more specific connection."

> "Your task is to justify your decision to a skeptical audience. What do they need to believe before they will accept your conclusion? Address that directly."

**Too easy (Grade 10 level):**
> "Write two reasons why you agree or disagree. Use the sentence frames."

**Too easy (Grade 11 level):**
> "Write a 2-paragraph explanation of your position addressed to someone who might disagree."

---

## Tone

Expects full intellectual engagement. Prompts should feel like real tasks — recommendations that will be read, arguments that will be evaluated, decisions that have stakes. Not a compliance form. Not a worksheet. The register should match the highest functional writing demands students will face next year.
