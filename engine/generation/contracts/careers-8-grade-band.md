# Careers 8 Grade-Band Contract

## Intended grade and course
Grade 8 Career Education in British Columbia.

This course is exploratory, developmental, and identity-building. It is about helping students notice strengths, habits, choices, community roles, technology risks, workplace readiness, and future possibilities in age-appropriate ways.

It is **not**:
- senior-secondary pathway planning
- polished adult career reflection
- resume/interview training for older students
- abstract personal philosophy
- workplace-prep writing in an adult professional tone

Students at this band are still learning how to:
- describe themselves clearly
- notice patterns in their choices and habits
- connect examples to bigger ideas
- explain short reasons
- make one realistic next-step plan

## Acceptable abstraction level
Default to concrete framing first.

### Strong fit
- personal strengths, interests, habits, values, preferences, and choices
- school, hobbies, sports, clubs, family, online life, and community examples
- visible cause/effect
- simple compare/contrast
- short explanation of what fits, what matters, or what connects
- one inferential step when the task is scaffolded

### Allowed with support
- self-concept
- mindset
- bias
- influence
- purpose
- contribution
- identity
- pathways
- digital footprint
- misinformation
- stereotypes

### Too abstract unless heavily grounded
- philosophical meaning questions with no concrete anchor
- broad labor-market or social theory
- mature identity analysis with no examples
- abstract future-planning language that assumes adult self-knowledge
- synthesis across multiple invisible concepts without setup

### Build rule
If students are asked to infer a pattern, describe a future connection, or explain what something means, they should first have concrete material such as:
- a list
- a sort
- a ranking task
- a scenario
- a role map
- a comparison prompt
- a visible example set

## Acceptable sentence length
Student-facing language should be short enough to hear once and use.

### Targets
- slide prompts: 6–14 words
- student instructions: 8–18 words
- reflection prompts: 8–20 words
- sentence frames: short and usable, not adult-sounding

### Avoid
- paragraph-length setup in student-facing space
- long “why we are doing this” explanations
- multi-condition prompts with buried instructions
- teacher-explainer tone inside student tasks

## Expected output length
Typical output at this band is short and structured.

### Strong fit
- 1–3 words for sorts, rankings, or labels
- 1 sentence for quick reflection
- 2–4 sentences for explanation
- short bullet lists
- simple graphic organizer entries
- one short paragraph when clearly scaffolded

### Not a default fit
- extended multi-paragraph reflection
- essay-like justification
- mature recommendation/proposal writing
- long open-ended abstract responses

### Output-demand rule
Careers 8 tasks should usually ask students to:
- notice
- choose
- sort
- compare
- match
- explain briefly
- connect to one real example
- identify one next step

### Comfortable vocabulary
- strengths

- values
- habits
- choices
- goals
- community
- work
- teamwork
- responsibility
- safety
- online identity
- influence
- stereotypes

### Use with visible support or examples
- self-concept
- mindset

- algorithm

- social influence

### Avoid as default student-facing language
- metacognition

- labour-market dynamics
- identity formation

- polished framework language adults say to adults
- counseling-style abstraction
- senior-secondary planning voice

## What “too easy” looks like
A Careers 8 task is too easy when it:
- asks obvious either/or questions with no reasoning
- uses fake-choice reflection where every answer is equally shallow
- asks “What job do you want?” with no support
- becomes moralizing instead of thoughtful
- can be completed with one-word answers that never become explanation
- uses generic “be yourself” or “follow your passion” language
- gives students almost no real decision, comparison, or connection work

## What “too advanced” looks like
A Careers 8 task is too advanced when it:
- sounds like senior-secondary reflection
- asks abstract questions with no concrete examples
- uses adult recommendation/proposal logic
- explains the pedagogy instead of the task
- expects synthesis across several invisible concepts without scaffold
- asks for essay-like justification
- assumes mature civic, workplace, or identity vocabulary
- sounds polished, balanced, or therapeutic instead of classroom-real

## Anchor examples

### Good sample prompt 1
Which of these feels most like you when group work is going well: organizer, encourager, problem-solver, idea starter, or finisher? Pick one and give one real example.

**Why it fits:**  
Concrete choices, school-based context, short explanation demand.

### Good sample prompt 2
Look at these three community needs. Which one matters most in your area right now? What jobs or roles help with it?

**Why it fits:**  
Concrete choice, local context, visible career connection.

### Good sample prompt 3
Name one digital risk people your age actually deal with. Who gets affected? Who helps respond?

**Why it fits:**  
Relevant, current, structured, and grounded in real experience.

## Non-negotiable tone rules
Student-facing Careers 8 materials should be:
- age-respectful
- concrete before abstract
- reflective, but not sentimental
- future-facing, but light
- classroom-real
- direct and usable

They should **not** be:
- childish
- slogan-like
- framework-heavy
- over-polished
- senior-secondary in tone
- adult counseling voice

## Build guardrail
A Careers 8 artifact is on-band when a student can complete it by using:
- a real example
- a short reason
- a simple comparison
- a visible choice
- a concrete connection

A Careers 8 artifact is drifting off-band when success depends on:
- abstract self-analysis
- extended justification
- hidden genre knowledge
- mature formal tone
- broad social theory
- unwritten future-planning assumptions

## Quick calibration test
Before approving a Careers 8 student-facing prompt, check:

1. Can a Grade 8 student picture the situation?
2. Does the task ask for one clear thinking move, not three hidden ones?
3. Is there a concrete example, list, scenario, or organizer if abstraction appears?
4. Does the wording sound like a real teacher talking to Grade 8 students?
5. Would this still make sense if read aloud once?

If the answer to two or more is no, the prompt is probably off-band.
