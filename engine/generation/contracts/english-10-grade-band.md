# Grade-Band Contract: English 10

**Course:** English Language Arts 10 (BC curriculum)  
**Engine theme:** `english_language_arts`  
**Typical architecture:** `multi_day_sequence`

---

## Who this student is

Grade 10. Beginning to work with more formal writing expectations but still needs visible structure and support. Capable of forming and supporting an opinion. Reads for information with guidance. Beginning to write for audiences beyond the classroom, but still needs the audience made explicit. Transitions from guided practice to more independent drafting with scaffolding still present.

---

## Acceptable abstraction level

**Moderate.** Students can reason from evidence if the evidence is provided or gathered through structured tasks. Can make one- or two-step inferences. Can explain "why" with support. Abstract tasks need practical grounding — e.g., workplace communication, school communication, real scenarios.

**Not acceptable:** multi-source synthesis without scaffolding, or reasoning demands that belong in Grade 11/12 (independently constructing a complex argument from scratch, formal analytical essay without frames).

---

## Acceptable sentence length

- **Prompt sentences:** 20–30 words. Can handle slightly more complexity than Grade 8 but still benefits from clarity.
- **Instructions:** 12–20 words per item.
- **Student output sentences:** full sentences expected, not just phrases. Paragraphs appropriate for writing tasks.

---

## Expected output length

- **Daily tasks:** 3–6 lines, full sentences expected
- **Short written responses:** 1–2 focused paragraphs
- **Exit tickets:** 3–5 lines
- **Final response:** 2–4 paragraphs, structured (may use frames)

Writing should be more complete than Grade 8 but does not need to be polished academic prose. Functional clarity is the standard.

---

## Acceptable vocabulary range

**Use:** functional communication vocabulary, workplace/school register terms, basic argument language (claim, reason, evidence, example). Can introduce terms like "audience," "purpose," "tone," and "register" if briefly explained.

**Avoid:** literary criticism vocabulary without grounding (discourse, rhetorical, analytical), senior-secondary academic register (synthesis, nuanced argumentation, theoretical lens).

---

## What "too easy" looks like

- Prompts that only ask students to copy or list without explaining
- Tasks that don't require a student to apply the lesson's skill
- Writing with so many frames filled in that the student adds only one or two words
- No audience awareness required

---

## What "too advanced" looks like

- Independent multi-paragraph formal argument with no frame support
- Vocabulary and reasoning demands at the Grade 12 proposal/recommendation level
- Abstract analysis without a concrete anchor task
- Expecting students to independently synthesize multiple sources

---

## Anchor examples — prompts at the right level

**Good:**
> "Write a short workplace update (3–4 sentences) explaining what you completed, what is next, and one thing your supervisor should know."

> "Read the scenario. What is the writer's main point? What evidence do they use? Do you find it convincing? Explain in 2–3 sentences."

> "Write a claim and two reasons. Use the sentence frames if you need them."

**Too easy:**
> "Do you agree or disagree? Write one sentence."

**Too advanced:**
> "Construct a formal argument analyzing the rhetorical strategies used to persuade different audience segments."

---

## Tone

Practical and clear. Feels like a functional writing course with real audiences and purposes. Not a literary analysis class, not a career brochure. Students should feel like they are learning to communicate effectively — at school, at work, in their community.
