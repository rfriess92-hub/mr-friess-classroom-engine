## Source Use Rules

Use sources to support careful thinking.

Do:

- identify the concept being used
- connect the source idea to the task
- explain what the evidence does and does not show
- use neutral examples

Do not:

- diagnose anyone

- use personal stories as required evidence

## Key Source Ideas

### Source Idea 1: Psychology Studies Behaviour and Mental Processes

Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes.

Behaviour means actions that can be observed.

Mental processes include internal experiences such as thinking, remembering, feeling, perceiving, and deciding.

Use this idea when you need to explain what psychology studies.

### Source Idea 2: Evidence Matters

Psychologists use evidence to support claims about behaviour and mental processes.

Evidence can come from observation, measurement, surveys, experiments, or other research methods.

Evidence helps separate careful explanation from opinion or guessing.

Use this idea when you need to explain why a claim needs support.

### Source Idea 3: Observation Is Not the Same as Inference

An observation is what can be directly noticed.

An inference is a possible explanation for what was observed.

A good inference should be supported by evidence, but it should not be treated as proven unless the evidence is strong enough.

Use this idea when you analyze a scenario.

### Source Idea 4: Correlation Is Not Causation

A correlation means two things are related.

A correlation does not prove that one thing caused the other.

Other factors may help explain the relationship.

Use this idea when a claim says one thing caused another without enough evidence.

### Source Idea 5: Research Ethics Protect Participants

Psychological research should treat participants responsibly.

Ethical research considers consent, privacy, fairness, potential harm, and responsible use of information.

Use this idea when judging whether a research plan is responsible.

## Vocabulary Support

| Term | Student-Friendly Definition | Use It When... |
|---|---|---|
| Psychology | The scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. | defining the course or field |
| Behaviour | Actions that can be observed. | describing what a person does |
| Mental processes | Internal experiences such as thoughts, memories, feelings, and perceptions. | explaining what cannot be directly seen |
| Evidence | Information used to support a claim. | deciding whether a claim is strong |
| Observation | Something directly noticed. | separating facts from guesses |
| Inference | A possible explanation. | explaining what might be happening |
| Correlation | A relationship between two things. | reading survey or pattern claims |
| Causation | One thing producing a change in another. | evaluating cause-and-effect claims |
| Ethics | Principles for responsible research and treatment of people. | judging research design |
