# Cycle A Assessment Blueprint: Scientific / Evidence

## 1. Purpose

This blueprint defines the assessment plan for Cycle A before any student assessment pack, teacher marking guide, answer key, or renderable classroom artifact is generated.

Cycle A answers the core question: How do psychologists know what they claim to know?

The assessment goal is to make students practise the habits that later cycles depend on: define terms accurately, separate observation from inference, identify claims, evaluate evidence, recognize limits, distinguish correlation from causation, and use psychological language responsibly.

## 2. Scope Control

This file is not the student assessment pack. It is not the teacher marking guide. It does not contain final quizzes, final answer keys, full rubrics, slide content, or printable worksheets.

This file decides what needs to be built next.

## 3. Cycle A Assessment Priorities

| Priority | What Students Must Show | Where It Appears |
|---|---|---|
| Concept accuracy | Define psychology, behaviour, mental process, claim, evidence, inference, opinion, variable, operational definition, correlation, causation, bias, ethics, and limitation. | Vocabulary checks, quiz, capstone. |
| Evidence reasoning | Separate claim, evidence, inference, opinion, correlation, causation, and limitation. | Claim/evidence/limit organizer, source analysis, capstone. |
| Method awareness | Explain why method, sample, variable definition, and ethical design affect claim strength. | Research-method mini-task, fictional ethics board task. |
| Responsible language | Avoid diagnosis, pop-psychology claims, personality-quiz logic, unsupported interpretation, and brain/body determinism. | Scenario tasks, feedback, capstone rubric. |
| Transfer | Apply the same evidence habit to research examples, brain-body claims, media claims, and everyday psychology claims. | Scenario tasks, media claim audit, capstone. |

## 8. Capstone Blueprint

Cycle A capstone: Evaluate a psychological claim.

Student-facing task should ask students to:

1. State the psychological claim.
2. Identify key vocabulary needed to understand the claim.
3. Identify the type of evidence being used.
4. Explain what the evidence supports.
5. Explain what the evidence does not prove.
6. Identify possible correlation/causation or third-variable issues where relevant.
7. Identify ethical or responsibility concerns.
8. Write a careful conclusion using responsible psychology language.

Acceptable claim sources:

- teacher-provided media claim
- short public-facing article excerpt
- fictional school/community claim
- simplified research summary
- psychology myth or misconception statement

Do not use:

- claims requiring students to disclose private experience
- diagnosis of classmates, family members, celebrities, or fictional characters
- true-crime cases
- therapy advice claims
- personality quiz results
- unsupported social media hot takes without a bounded analysis frame

## 10. Concrete Classroom Texture Required

When building the actual student pack, avoid a sterile worksheet sequence. Cycle A should feel like students are learning to inspect claims in the world around them.

Include:

- short psychology myth cards
- pop-psychology claim sorting
- media headline audits
- fictional mini research proposals
- neutral school/community claim examples
- quick misconception moments
- short discussion prompts that ask, What can we responsibly say?
- repeated prompts asking, What would better evidence look like?

Avoid:

- long generic note-taking worksheets
- abstract tables with no classroom scenario
- overly technical research-method vocabulary before students need it
- personal surveys about students or classmates
- pseudo-therapy reflection prompts

## 11. Safety and Visibility Requirements

Before any Cycle A student-facing assessment artifact is built, confirm that it:

- contains no answer key
- contains no teacher rationale or hidden scoring notes
- does not ask students to disclose personal mental health, family history, trauma, relationship history, diagnosis, or therapy experience
- does not ask students to diagnose or analyze peers, teachers, family members, celebrities, or fictional characters as if they were clinicians
- uses fictional, neutral, public, or source-based examples
- asks students to state limits, not just claims
- uses careful language around brain/body explanations
- keeps teacher-facing supports in a separate marking guide

## 13. Review Gate Before Student Pack

Before building the student assessment pack, review this blueprint for three questions:

1. Does it still feel like the original Psychology course vision, or has it become generic?
2. Are the assessment pieces varied enough to avoid a repetitive worksheet feel?
3. Are student-facing and teacher-facing boundaries clear enough to prevent answer leakage and unsafe disclosure?

If the answer to any question is no, revise this blueprint before producing classroom-facing materials.
