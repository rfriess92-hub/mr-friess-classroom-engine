# Cycle A Foundations Marking Guide

## Assessment Overview

This marking guide supports the Cycle A Foundations assessment. The assessment checks whether students can define psychology, distinguish evidence from opinion, separate observation from inference, explain correlation and causation carefully, and identify a basic ethical concern.

This is teacher-facing only. It must not render into the student assessment packet.

## Learning Targets Assessed

Students should demonstrate that they can:

- define psychology accurately
- use evidence language
- separate observation from inference
- explain why correlation does not prove causation
- identify a possible third factor
- name an ethical or fairness issue in a simple research design
- write a cautious explanation that avoids overclaiming

## Answer Key and Look-Fors

### 1. Psychology

Look for: psychology as the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes.

Acceptable answer:

> Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes.

Partial answer:

> Psychology is the study of behaviour.

This is partly correct but missing mental processes and/or scientific method.

### 2. Evidence

Look for: evidence helps psychologists support claims, test ideas, avoid guessing, and move beyond opinion.

### 3. Observation and Inference

Observation example:

> The student looked at the clock three times.

Inference example:

> The student might be bored, waiting for class to end, worried about time, or trying to manage time.

Important: students should not treat the inference as proven.

### 4. Claim Check

Look for: the claim says music improves memory or causes students to remember more.

### 5. Evidence Needed

Look for: comparison data, controlled conditions, memory test results, information about study time, type of music, student differences, or repeated trials.

### 6. Caution

Look for: other variables could explain the result; music may help some students and distract others; the claim needs evidence before it can be accepted.

### 7. Correlation

Look for: sleep hours and reported focus are related.

### 8. Causation

Look for: no, the survey does not prove causation. It shows a relationship, but other variables could be involved.

### 9. Third Factor

Possible answers:

- stress
- nutrition
- health
- workload
- screen use
- classroom environment
- motivation
- prior sleep quality

### 10. Ethical Check

Look for: fairness, consent, equal opportunity to learn, privacy, not disadvantaging a class, or responsible use of student data.

### 11. Better Design

Possible answers:

- get consent or explain the purpose clearly
- give both groups access to review support eventually
- rotate conditions
- compare students to their own previous performance
- protect student privacy
- avoid using grades in a way that disadvantages one group

### 12. Careful Explanation

Strong response should say that the review sheet is associated with higher scores, but the scenario alone does not prove it caused the higher scores. Students may explain that review sheets could help memory or preparation, but other factors may also matter.

### 13. Limit Statement

Look for: students cannot know from this scenario alone whether the review sheet caused higher scores, whether students who completed it were already more prepared, or whether another factor explains the pattern.

## Rubric

| Criteria | Emerging | Developing | Proficient | Extending |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Concept accuracy | Key terms are missing or inaccurate. | Some terms are accurate, but explanations are incomplete. | Key terms are accurate and used appropriately. | Key terms are accurate and applied carefully to new examples. |
| Evidence reasoning | Responses rely mostly on opinion or guessing. | Responses mention evidence but do not explain its role clearly. | Responses explain why evidence is needed before making claims. | Responses explain evidence limits and name possible alternative explanations. |
| Correlation and causation | Response treats a relationship as proof. | Response shows partial awareness that a relationship is not proof. | Response clearly explains that correlation does not prove causation. | Response adds a plausible third factor or improved research question. |
| Ethics | Ethical issue is missing or vague. | Ethical issue is partly relevant. | Ethical issue is relevant and connected to the scenario. | Response suggests a practical improvement that protects fairness or privacy. |
| Communication | Response is unclear or incomplete. | Response is understandable but uneven. | Response is clear and uses course vocabulary. | Response is clear, precise, and cautious about what can be known. |

## Feedback Language

Use short, specific comments.

Examples:

- You identified the relationship, but you need to explain why it does not prove causation.
- Strong evidence language. You separated what is observed from what is inferred.
- Be more cautious: the scenario does not prove what someone is thinking.
- Good ethical concern. Add how the researcher could make the design fairer.
- Accurate definition. Next step: apply the definition to a new example.

## Common Misconceptions

Students may:

- define psychology as advice or therapy only
- treat an inference as a fact
- assume a correlation proves a cause
- forget to mention evidence
- describe ethics as only being “nice” instead of protecting participants
- overstate what a simple scenario can prove

## Accommodation Notes

Possible supports:

- read prompts aloud
- allow point-form responses
- provide vocabulary bank
- reduce number of constructed-response items while preserving targets
- allow oral conference for explanation items
- provide sentence frames for correlation and causation

## Teacher Use Notes

This assessment is best used after students have practised observation, inference, evidence, perspectives, research questions, variables, correlation, causation, and ethics.

Do not penalize students for not using advanced terminology beyond the Cycle A target. Focus on cautious reasoning, evidence language, and accurate foundational concepts.
