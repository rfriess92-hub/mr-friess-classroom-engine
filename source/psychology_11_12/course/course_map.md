# Psychology 11/12 Course Map

## 1. Purpose

This course map converts the A-F Psychology Lens Matrix into a teachable planning sequence. It does not replace the lens matrix and does not generate the full classroom package yet. It translates the matrix into a course-level map: cycle sequence, recurring routines, module order, artifact expectations, assessment rhythm, and safety controls.

The course remains a lens-based spiral curriculum. Each cycle has one dominant lens, and each cycle revisits the major domains of psychology through that lens. The goal is coherence without turning the course into either a rigid textbook march or a random topic grab bag.

## 2. Course Stance

Psychology 11/12 is an academic, classroom-safe, evidence-focused senior psychology course. Students should learn to think like psychology students: define concepts accurately, separate observation from inference, evaluate claims, recognize limits, use careful language, and apply psychology responsibly.

The course is not therapy, diagnosis, peer counselling, personality sorting, self-help advice, or true-crime entertainment. Student-facing work should use fictional scenarios, public examples, neutral case studies, source analysis, and low-risk academic reflection. Teacher-facing materials may include safety notes, answer keys, misconceptions, differentiation, and marking support, but those supports must stay separate from student-facing artifacts.

## 3. Whole-Course Structure

| Cycle | Lens | Core Question | Course Function | Build Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Scientific / Evidence | How do psychologists know what they claim to know? | Establishes claim analysis, research methods, ethics, evidence, and limits. | Build first. |
| B | Intellectual / Learning | How do humans learn, think, create, and adapt? | Builds learning, memory, cognition, motivation, creativity, and applied performance supports. | Build after Cycle A. |
| C | Relational / Identity | How are humans shaped by connection, belonging, sexuality, personality, and social context? | Builds social, relational, identity, personality, and sexuality psychology with strong disclosure controls. | Build after Cycle B. |
| D | Wellness / Change | How do people cope, struggle, change, and access support? | Builds stress, coping, mental health literacy, treatment awareness, support systems, and behaviour change. | Build after Cycle C. |
| E | Justice / Responsibility | How should psychology be used when harm, safety, memory, law, and accountability are involved? | Maps forensic, legal, memory, bias, risk, and responsibility applications. | Mapped future cycle until active files exist. |
| F | Systems / Media / Influence | How are humans shaped by designed environments, media, persuasion, and technology? | Maps media, persuasion, attention, misinformation, consumer psychology, technology, AI, and ethical design. | Mapped future cycle until active files exist. |

## 4. Permanent Through-Lines

These through-lines are not separate units. They are recurring checks in every cycle, major unit, capstone, and discussion structure.

| Through-Line | Planning Question | Required Use |
|---|---|---|
| Cultural / Contextual | How do culture, community, identity, language, power, family, environment, and access shape this? | Prevents treating one group as the default human norm. |
| Developmental / Lifespan | How does this change across childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and older adulthood? | Prevents students from assuming one explanation applies equally across all life stages. |
| Biological / Brain-Body | What brain, body, nervous system, endocrine, hormonal, sleep, stress, or biological systems are involved, and what are the limits of that explanation? | Grounds psychology in biology without reducing people to biology. |
| Ethical / Evidence | How do we know? What are the limits? What responsibilities come with this knowledge? | Keeps the course academic, safe, and evidence-focused. |

## 5. Repeating Course Routine

Each cycle should use a predictable intellectual routine so students learn how psychology works as a discipline. The content changes by lens, but the reasoning pattern should remain stable.

| Routine Layer | Student Habit | Example Artifact |
|---|---|---|
| Anchor Question | Name the lens and the psychology problem. | Cycle opener, slide deck, student packet front page. |
| Concept Accuracy | Define terms carefully and avoid overclaiming. | Vocabulary checks, concept cards, misconception sorts. |
| Evidence Check | Separate claim, evidence, inference, opinion, correlation, causation, and limit. | Source analysis, research-method mini-task, claim audit. |
| Application | Apply concepts to fictional, neutral, or public examples. | Scenario analysis, case file, media example, design audit. |
| Through-Line Check | Revisit culture/context, development, brain-body, and ethics/evidence. | Exit ticket, seminar prep, graphic organizer. |
| Synthesis | Combine multiple domains through the cycle lens. | Capstone task, short presentation, analysis packet. |

## 6. Assessment Rhythm

Assessment should repeat across cycles so students are not learning a new assessment system every time. The course should use stable assessment layers with changing content.

| Assessment Layer | Purpose | Frequency Guideline | Audience Boundary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vocabulary and concept checks | Build accurate disciplinary language. | Weekly or per module. | Student-facing. |
| Scenario applications | Apply psychology to fictional or neutral examples. | Every module. | Student-facing. |
| Source/evidence analysis | Distinguish claim, evidence, limit, correlation, causation, and inference. | At least twice per cycle. | Student-facing. |
| Short quizzes/tests | Check foundational knowledge. | One or two per cycle. | Student-facing; answer keys teacher-only. |
| Applied mini-project | Use psychology responsibly in a realistic setting. | One per cycle where useful. | Student-facing task; teacher-only rubric separate. |
| Capstone/performance task | Synthesize domains through the cycle lens. | One per cycle. | Student-facing packet and teacher-facing marking guide must stay separate. |

## 7. Artifact Family Per Cycle

Each cycle should eventually produce the same artifact family. This keeps planning consistent and protects the boundary between student-facing and teacher-facing materials.

| Artifact | Audience | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Teacher binder | Teacher | Delivery guide, pacing, safety notes, misconceptions, differentiation, answer keys where appropriate. |
| Student packet | Student | Core readings, activities, scenario applications, vocabulary, and reflection structures. No answer keys. |
| Slide deck | Student | Low-density classroom instruction and discussion prompts. No teacher-only rationale. |
| Assessment pack | Student | Quizzes, performance tasks, capstone instructions, and student-facing rubrics where appropriate. |
| Marking guide | Teacher | Rubrics, look-fors, answer keys, feedback language, accommodations, and grading support. |
| Source sheet | Student | Short traceable concept support. Not a textbook replacement. |
| Capstone packet | Student | Synthesis task specific to the cycle lens. |
| QA bundle | Teacher/repo | Artifact inventory, visibility checks, answer-leakage checks, and known issues. |

## 8. Cycle A Course Map: Scientific / Evidence

Core question: How do psychologists know what they claim to know?

Primary role: Cycle A establishes the discipline. Students learn to separate observation, inference, claim, evidence, opinion, correlation, causation, and ethical limits.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | What is psychology? | Psychology as science; evidence; applied psychology. | Psychology vs advice/common sense/pop psychology sort. | Avoid treating psychology as self-help or personality quizzes. |
| A2 | Claims, evidence, and limits | Research methods; variables; operational definitions; sampling. | Claim/evidence/limit organizer using neutral examples. | Do not ask students to research classmates' private lives. |
| A3 | Correlation, causation, and bias | Cognition; inference; research design; third variables. | Correlation vs causation scenario set. | Keep examples low-risk and non-shaming. |
| A4 | Brain-body claims | Brain/body; behaviour; biological explanations. | Evaluate simplified brain-behaviour claims for overclaiming. | Avoid brain-based determinism. |
| A5 | Ethics and responsible research | Consent; privacy; risk; benefit; debriefing. | Fictional ethics board review. | Use fictional studies only. |
| A6 | Psychological claims in media | Applied psychology; evidence quality; source credibility. | Media psychology claim audit. | Avoid diagnosing public figures. |

Cycle A capstone: Evaluate a psychological claim. Students identify the claim, evidence, limits, possible correlation/causation issues, ethical concerns, and a careful conclusion.

Cycle A build note: This should be the first fully normalized cycle because it teaches the reasoning routines used by all later cycles.

## 9. Cycle B Course Map: Intellectual / Learning

Core question: How do humans learn, think, create, and adapt?

Primary role: Cycle B studies learning, memory, cognition, motivation, creativity, intelligence, education, and applied skill development.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| B1 | Brain, attention, and learning | Brain/body; attention; sleep; neuroplasticity. | Explain how a fictional learner improves a skill. | Avoid fixed-brain or deficit framing. |
| B2 | Learning theory | Classical conditioning; operant conditioning; reinforcement; feedback. | Build a practice plan for a fictional skill. | Avoid manipulative behaviour-control framing. |
| B3 | Memory and study | Encoding; retrieval; working memory; long-term memory; forgetting. | Compare weak and strong study strategies. | Avoid public ranking of student ability. |
| B4 | Cognition, intelligence, creativity | Problem-solving; bias; creativity; intelligence. | Creativity/problem-solving challenge with evidence reflection. | Treat intelligence testing critically and carefully. |
| B5 | Motivation and development | Executive function; adolescence; persistence; achievement. | Case study of an adolescent learner and support design. | Avoid deficit framing. |
| B6 | Learning systems and equity | Peer effects; teacher expectations; stereotype threat; access. | Analyze how context affects a fictional learner. | Avoid putting students on the spot about identity, class, or ability. |

Cycle B capstone: Design an evidence-based learning or performance support for a fictional person, team, class, or workplace. Students explain brain/body, learning, memory, motivation, context, evidence, and ethics.

## 10. Cycle C Course Map: Relational / Identity

Core question: How are humans shaped by connection, belonging, sexuality, personality, and social context?

Primary role: Cycle C studies relationships, identity, sexuality, personality, belonging, social interpretation, and the relational shaping of behaviour.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | Social brain and belonging | Brain/body; reward; threat; bonding; regulation. | Explain a fictional social stress response. | Avoid reducing relationships to chemicals. |
| C2 | Relational memory and interpretation | Memory; emotion; expectations; schema. | Analyze how past interactions shape a fictional character's interpretation. | Do not ask for relationship histories. |
| C3 | Attribution and social cognition | Attribution; inference; mind-reading errors; assumptions. | Relationship misunderstanding scenario. | Use fictional scenarios. |
| C4 | Personality and identity | Traits; temperament; self-concept; identity. | Compare personality explanations without labelling people. | Avoid public personality sorting. |
| C5 | Sexuality, consent, and communication | Puberty; hormones; attraction; consent; boundaries. | Consent and communication case analysis. | Academic, respectful, non-disclosure-based framing. |
| C6 | Culture, norms, and belonging | Culture; prejudice; gender norms; family/community context. | Fictional school/community belonging analysis. | Avoid forced personal identity disclosure. |

Cycle C capstone: Analyze a fictional relationship, media scene, or public scenario using brain/body, memory, cognition, personality, culture, development, ethics, and evidence. The goal is careful explanation without diagnosis or overclaiming.

## 11. Cycle D Course Map: Wellness / Change

Core question: How do people cope, struggle, change, and access support?

Primary role: Cycle D studies stress, health, coping, mental health literacy, treatment awareness, resilience, support systems, and responsible help-seeking.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Stress and the body | Stress response; nervous system; endocrine system; sleep; health. | Explain the stress response in a fictional scenario. | Avoid trauma disclosure. |
| D2 | Habits, avoidance, and coping | Learning; reinforcement; avoidance; coping patterns. | Behaviour-change plan for a fictional low-risk habit. | Do not ask students to disclose personal struggles. |
| D3 | Appraisal and rumination | Cognition; problem-solving; appraisal; thought patterns. | Compare helpful and unhelpful coping thoughts in scenarios. | Keep examples mild and fictional. |
| D4 | Emotion regulation and resilience | Emotion; motivation; support; resilience. | Emotion-regulation strategy analysis. | Do not make it therapy practice. |
| D5 | Mental health literacy | Distress vs disorder; diagnosis boundaries; stigma; impairment. | Myth-busting source analysis. | No self-diagnosis or peer diagnosis. |
| D6 | Treatment and support systems | Therapy approaches; medication literacy; school/community supports; referral pathways. | Support-awareness resource. | No crisis role-play or peer counselling. |

Cycle D capstone: Create a mental health literacy or support-awareness product, or analyze a fictional case with clear boundaries: concept accuracy, stigma reduction, support literacy, and appropriate referral awareness.

## 12. Cycle E Course Map: Justice / Responsibility

Core question: How should psychology be used when harm, safety, memory, law, and accountability are involved?

Primary role: Cycle E maps legal and forensic applications of psychology with strong attention to evidence, memory reliability, bias, risk, responsibility, and ethics.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| E1 | Decision-making under stress | Brain/body; threat; arousal; impulse control. | Analyze decision-making under stress in a sanitized case. | Avoid graphic harm details. |
| E2 | Eyewitness memory | Memory; false memory; confidence; misinformation. | Eyewitness reliability case analysis. | Use sanitized or fictional cases. |
| E3 | Bias and legal judgment | Cognition; attribution; stereotyping; moral reasoning. | Legal judgment bias scenarios. | Avoid inflammatory real-world debates unless carefully framed. |
| E4 | Authority, conformity, and group decisions | Social psychology; authority; obedience; conformity; jury dynamics. | Fictional low-risk jury deliberation simulation. | Avoid true-crime entertainment. |
| E5 | Development, risk, and responsibility | Adolescent development; risk-taking; accountability; rehabilitation. | Case study on adolescent responsibility. | Avoid deterministic language about youth. |
| E6 | Mental health, law, and ethics | Competency; responsibility; stigma; expert testimony; confidentiality. | Forensic ethics board review. | No diagnosis of real people. |

Cycle E capstone: Analyze a sanitized fictional legal/forensic case file. Students explain what psychology can and cannot responsibly conclude using memory, cognition, social influence, development, evidence, and ethics.

Cycle E build note: Keep as a mapped future cycle until active source files exist.

## 13. Cycle F Course Map: Systems / Media / Influence

Core question: How are humans shaped by designed environments, media, persuasion, and technology?

Primary role: Cycle F maps attention, behaviour design, media systems, persuasion, misinformation, social media, consumer psychology, AI, and ethical design.

| Module Order | Module Focus | Major Domains | Suggested Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | Reward, novelty, and attention | Brain/body; reward; attention; habit loops; arousal. | Analyze a notification or app design. | Avoid shaming students for technology use. |
| F2 | Behaviour design | Learning; cues; rewards; friction; reinforcement. | Redesign a system to support autonomy. | Do not teach manipulation as the objective. |
| F3 | Misinformation and source confusion | Memory; cognition; fluency; cognitive load. | Media misinformation audit. | Keep politically sensitive examples neutral if needed. |
| F4 | Social comparison and identity signalling | Social psychology; identity; parasocial relationships; norms. | Analyze social media identity pressures through a fictional example. | Avoid public disclosure of online habits. |
| F5 | Consumer psychology | Scarcity; branding; status; emotion; motivation. | Advertising analysis. | Avoid body-image shaming and manipulative targeting. |
| F6 | Ethics, AI, and persuasive systems | Dark patterns; consent; autonomy; algorithms; youth audiences. | Ethical design review. | Teach agency and design awareness, not moral panic. |

Cycle F capstone: Conduct a media, technology, advertising, gaming, or consumer-system audit. Students identify psychological mechanisms, evidence, ethical concerns, cultural/contextual factors, and design recommendations.

Cycle F build note: Keep as a mapped future cycle until active source files exist.

## 15. QA Rules For Future Package Builds

1. Every student-facing artifact must be free of teacher-only rationale, answer keys, hidden marking notes, and required private disclosure.
2. Every teacher-facing artifact must be clearly marked as teacher-facing.
3. Every cycle must include the four permanent through-lines.
4. Every capstone must synthesize more than one psychology domain through the cycle lens.
5. Cycle C and Cycle D require extra disclosure controls.
6. Cycle E must avoid graphic true-crime drift.
7. Cycle F must avoid moral panic and student shaming around technology.
8. OpenStax Psychology 2e remains the academic spine, but course artifacts should translate concepts into classroom-appropriate lessons rather than reproduce textbook material directly.
9. Assessment packs and marking guides must be created as separate artifacts when answer keys or teacher notes are involved.
10. Future source-register cleanup should remain separate from course-map and package-building PRs.
