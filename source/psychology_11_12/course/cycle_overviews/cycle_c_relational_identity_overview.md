# Cycle C Overview: Relational / Identity

## Core Question

How are humans shaped by connection, belonging, sexuality, personality, and social context?

## Primary Course Function

Cycle C studies relationships, identity, sexuality, personality, belonging, social interpretation, and the relational shaping of behaviour. It is a higher-disclosure-risk cycle and must preserve strict boundaries between academic analysis and personal exposure.

## Module Sequence

| Module | Focus | Major Domains | Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | Social brain and belonging | Brain/body; reward; threat; bonding; regulation. | Explain a fictional social stress response. | Avoid reducing relationships to chemicals. |
| C2 | Relational memory and interpretation | Memory; emotion; expectations; schema. | Analyze how past interactions shape a fictional character's interpretation. | Do not ask for relationship histories. |
| C3 | Attribution and social cognition | Attribution; inference; mind-reading errors; assumptions. | Relationship misunderstanding scenario. | Use fictional scenarios. |
| C4 | Personality and identity | Traits; temperament; self-concept; identity. | Compare personality explanations without labelling people. | Avoid public personality sorting. |
| C5 | Sexuality, consent, and communication | Puberty; hormones; attraction; consent; boundaries. | Consent and communication case analysis. | Academic, respectful, non-disclosure-based framing. |
| C6 | Culture, norms, and belonging | Culture; prejudice; gender norms; family/community context. | Fictional school/community belonging analysis. | Avoid forced personal identity disclosure. |

## Required Through-Line Prompts

| Through-Line | Prompt |
|---|---|
| Cultural / Contextual | How do culture, family, community, gender norms, power, and belonging shape this scenario? |
| Developmental / Lifespan | How might identity, sexuality, peer belonging, and relationship expectations change across age and life stage? |
| Biological / Brain-Body | What brain/body systems may be involved in connection, threat, attraction, rejection, or regulation, and what are the limits of that explanation? |
| Ethical / Evidence | What can psychology responsibly explain here, and where would diagnosis, labelling, or overinterpretation become unsafe? |

## Capstone Frame

Analyze a fictional relationship, media scene, or public scenario using brain/body, memory, cognition, personality, culture, development, ethics, and evidence. The goal is careful explanation without diagnosis or overclaiming.

## Student-Facing Artifact List

- Cycle opener / lens overview
- Fictional social stress scenario
- Relational memory and interpretation task
- Attribution and misunderstanding organizer
- Personality/identity explanation comparison
- Consent and communication case analysis
- Capstone packet

## Teacher-Facing Artifact List

- Teacher binder with disclosure controls and discussion protocols
- Safety notes for sexuality, identity, relationships, and belonging topics
- Misconception guide for personality, attachment-style language, and social-brain claims
- Marking guide and answer keys where appropriate
- QA bundle confirming no private disclosure or peer analysis is required

## Safety Controls

Cycle C should not ask students to disclose attachment style, relationship history, family conflict, sexuality, private identity struggle, or personal support systems. Student-facing tasks must rely on fictional scenarios, public media examples, neutral cases, and carefully bounded academic language.

## Active Source Status

Cycle C is an active external package not yet normalized into the lens-based architecture. It should be normalized after Cycle B.
