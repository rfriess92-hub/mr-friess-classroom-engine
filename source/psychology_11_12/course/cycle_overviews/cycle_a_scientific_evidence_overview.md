# Cycle A Overview: Scientific / Evidence

## Core Question

How do psychologists know what they claim to know?

## Primary Course Function

Cycle A establishes the discipline. Students learn to separate observation, inference, claim, evidence, opinion, correlation, causation, and ethical limits. This cycle should be built first because it teaches the reasoning routines that later cycles reuse.

## Module Sequence

| Module | Focus | Major Domains | Student Work | Safety / QA Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | What is psychology? | Psychology as science; evidence; applied psychology. | Psychology vs advice, common sense, and pop psychology sort. | Avoid treating psychology as self-help or personality quizzes. |
| A2 | Claims, evidence, and limits | Research methods; variables; operational definitions; sampling. | Claim/evidence/limit organizer using neutral examples. | Do not ask students to research classmates' private lives. |
| A3 | Correlation, causation, and bias | Cognition; inference; research design; third variables. | Correlation vs causation scenario set. | Keep examples low-risk and non-shaming. |
| A4 | Brain-body claims | Brain/body; behaviour; biological explanations. | Evaluate simplified brain-behaviour claims for overclaiming. | Avoid brain-based determinism. |
| A5 | Ethics and responsible research | Consent; privacy; risk; benefit; debriefing. | Fictional ethics board review. | Use fictional studies only. |
| A6 | Psychological claims in media | Applied psychology; evidence quality; source credibility. | Media psychology claim audit. | Avoid diagnosing public figures. |

## Required Through-Line Prompts

| Through-Line | Prompt |
|---|---|
| Cultural / Contextual | Whose behaviour, community, or context is represented in the evidence, and whose may be missing? |
| Developmental / Lifespan | Would this claim apply equally to adolescents, adults, and older adults? |
| Biological / Brain-Body | What biological explanation is being claimed, and what are the limits of that explanation? |
| Ethical / Evidence | What evidence supports the claim, what are the limits, and what responsibilities come with using it? |

## Capstone Frame

Evaluate a psychological claim. Students identify the claim, evidence, limits, possible correlation/causation issues, ethical concerns, and a careful conclusion.

## Student-Facing Artifact List

- Cycle opener / lens overview
- Vocabulary and concept checks
- Claim/evidence/limit organizer
- Correlation vs causation scenarios
- Fictional ethics board task
- Media claim audit
- Capstone packet

## Teacher-Facing Artifact List

- Teacher binder with pacing and delivery notes
- Misconception guide for science/evidence language
- Answer keys and marking guide
- Safety notes for claims, ethics, and media examples
- QA bundle confirming student-facing artifacts contain no teacher-only material

## Safety Controls

Cycle A is lower disclosure risk than later cycles, but it still must avoid unsupported interpretation, diagnosis, overclaiming, personality-quiz logic, and private student research. All applied examples should be fictional, public, neutral, or source-based.
