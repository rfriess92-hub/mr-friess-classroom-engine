# Psychology 11/12 Vocabulary Bank

## 1. Purpose

This vocabulary bank normalizes recurring Psychology 11/12 language before full classroom-facing packages are generated. It is a planning-source document, not a final student glossary.

The bank should support consistent language across cycles, assessments, slides, student packets, source sheets, and marking guides. Vocabulary should be taught as disciplinary tools for careful thinking, not as labels for students or people in their lives.

## 2. Usage Rules

1. Define terms in context. Do not teach vocabulary as isolated memorization only.
2. Pair concept vocabulary with evidence and limits vocabulary.
3. Do not use vocabulary to diagnose, rank, sort, or expose students.
4. Use fictional, neutral, public, sanitized, or source-based examples.
5. Keep teacher-only terms, answer keys, and misconception notes out of student-facing vocabulary sheets.
6. Revisit core terms across cycles rather than treating them as one-time definitions.

## 3. Course-Wide Core Terms

These terms recur across all cycles and should be introduced early, then reused throughout the course.

| Term | Planning Definition | Use Across Course | Safety / Language Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| psychology | The scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. | Course anchor. | Do not reduce psychology to advice, therapy, personality quizzes, or self-help. |
| behaviour | Observable action or response. | All cycles. | Separate observation from interpretation. |
| mental process | Internal process such as thought, emotion, memory, attention, motivation, or perception. | All cycles. | Avoid claiming certainty about internal states without evidence. |
| claim | A statement that says something is true or likely true. | Evidence checks and source analysis. | Ask what evidence supports it. |
| evidence | Information used to support or challenge a claim. | All assessments. | Evidence has quality, limits, and context. |
| inference | A conclusion drawn from evidence. | Scenario analysis. | Mark inference as inference, not fact. |
| opinion | A personal belief or judgment. | Claim/evidence sorting. | Opinions may be meaningful but are not the same as evidence. |
| variable | A factor that can change or be measured. | Research-method tasks. | Define variables operationally. |
| operational definition | A precise definition of how a variable is measured or identified. | Cycle A and research tasks. | Prevents vague claims. |
| correlation | A relationship between variables. | Cycle A and evidence analysis. | Correlation does not prove causation. |
| causation | Evidence that one factor produces change in another. | Evidence analysis. | Requires stronger research design than correlation. |
| bias | A systematic influence that can distort interpretation, judgment, or results. | Research, cognition, social, legal, media. | Do not use as an insult. |
| context | Conditions surrounding behaviour or mental processes. | All through-line checks. | Prevents one-size-fits-all explanation. |
| culture | Shared meanings, practices, values, identities, and social patterns. | Cultural/contextual through-line. | Avoid treating one group as the default. |
| development | Change across age, experience, and life stage. | Developmental/lifespan through-line. | Avoid assuming one explanation applies equally to all ages. |
| brain-body system | Biological systems involved in behaviour and mental processes. | Biological/brain-body through-line. | Avoid biological determinism. |
| ethics | Responsibilities involved in studying, applying, and communicating psychology. | All cycles. | Includes privacy, consent, risk, harm, stigma, and limits. |
| limitation | A boundary on what evidence or a claim can support. | All assessments. | Strong psychology names limits clearly. |

## 4. Evidence and Research Vocabulary

Primary cycle: A. Reused in every cycle.

| Term | Planning Definition | Primary Uses | Safety / Language Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| research method | A structured way to study a question. | Cycle A, source analysis. | Match claim strength to method. |
| sample | The people, animals, cases, or data included in a study. | Evidence quality. | Ask who is represented and who is missing. |
| population | The larger group a study may try to describe. | Generalization. | Avoid overgeneralizing beyond the sample. |
| hypothesis | A testable prediction. | Research design tasks. | Not the same as a guess without structure. |
| experiment | A study design that manipulates a variable to test cause and effect. | Causation. | Requires ethical limits. |
| control group | A comparison group used to evaluate an intervention or condition. | Research methods. | Helps interpret effects. |
| random assignment | Assigning participants to conditions by chance. | Experimental design. | Supports causal inference. |
| survey | A method using questions to gather self-report data. | Research methods. | Self-report has limits. |
| case study | Detailed study of one person, group, or situation. | Research methods, applied examples. | Rich detail but limited generalization. |
| naturalistic observation | Observing behaviour in ordinary settings. | Research methods. | Observation still requires interpretation limits. |
| replication | Repeating a study to test whether findings hold. | Evidence quality. | One study is rarely enough. |
| peer review | Expert review before publication. | Source credibility. | Helpful but not a guarantee of truth. |
| informed consent | Agreement to participate based on clear information. | Ethics. | Central to responsible research. |
| confidentiality | Protecting private information. | Research ethics and support systems. | Student privacy is a classroom boundary. |
| debriefing | Explaining a study after participation, especially if deception was involved. | Ethics. | Connect to harm reduction. |

## 5. Learning, Cognition, and Motivation Vocabulary

Primary cycle: B. Reused in A, C, D, F.

| Term | Planning Definition | Primary Uses | Safety / Language Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| learning | A relatively lasting change in behaviour or knowledge based on experience. | Cycle B. | Avoid fixed-ability framing. |
| attention | Selection and focus of mental resources. | Learning, media, stress. | Context and design affect attention. |
| memory | Processes involved in encoding, storing, and retrieving information. | Learning, identity, eyewitness memory. | Memory is constructive, not a perfect recording. |
| encoding | Taking information into memory. | Study strategies. | Links to attention and meaning. |
| retrieval | Accessing stored information. | Study, testing, eyewitness memory. | Retrieval practice can support learning. |
| working memory | Limited mental workspace for holding and using information. | Learning, cognition, assessment supports. | Do not use to rank students. |
| long-term memory | More durable storage of knowledge and experiences. | Learning and memory. | Retrieval still depends on cues and context. |
| forgetting | Loss or inaccessibility of information over time. | Study and memory. | Normal process, not moral failure. |
| classical conditioning | Learning through association between stimuli. | Learning theory. | Avoid manipulative framing. |
| operant conditioning | Learning through consequences such as reinforcement or punishment. | Behaviour plans, systems, habits. | Use ethically; do not frame people as controllable objects. |
| reinforcement | A consequence that increases a behaviour. | Learning, habits, behaviour design. | Can be used supportively or manipulatively. |
| punishment | A consequence that decreases a behaviour. | Learning theory. | Discuss limits and unintended effects. |
| feedback | Information about performance or behaviour that guides adjustment. | Learning and assessment. | Effective feedback is specific and usable. |
| motivation | Processes that initiate, direct, and sustain behaviour. | Learning, wellness, consumer psychology. | Avoid treating motivation as character worth. |
| executive function | Cognitive processes used to plan, organize, inhibit, shift, and monitor behaviour. | Learning, development, stress. | Avoid deficit language. |
| creativity | Producing ideas or work that is novel and useful in context. | Cognition and learning. | Not only artistic talent. |
| intelligence | Capacity for reasoning, problem-solving, learning, or adaptation as defined by context and measurement. | Cycle B. | Treat testing critically and carefully. |

| self-concept | A person's understanding of who they are. | Identity and development. | Do not ask for private self-disclosure. |

| personality | Patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving. | Cycle C. | Avoid public personality sorting. |

| Term | Planning Definition | Primary Uses | Safety / Language Note |
|---|---|---|---|

| stressor | A demand or condition that may trigger stress. | Stress scenarios. | Use fictional or mild examples. |
| coping | Efforts to manage demands, emotions, or situations. | Wellness and change. | Do not ask students to disclose private coping. |

| rumination | Repetitive thinking about distress, causes, or consequences. | Cognition and wellness. | Do not invite private disclosure. |
| emotion regulation | Processes used to influence emotional experience or expression. | Wellness, relationships. | Teach as analysis, not therapy practice. |
| resilience | Adaptation or recovery in the face of challenge. | Wellness and development. | Avoid implying people should simply endure harm. |
| mental health literacy | Knowledge and attitudes that support accurate understanding, stigma reduction, and help-seeking. | Cycle D. | Not diagnosis training. |

| therapy | Professional mental health support using trained approaches. | Treatment awareness. | Do not simulate therapy in class. |

## 10. Assessment Vocabulary

These terms should appear across student-facing success criteria and teacher-facing marking guides.

| Term | Planning Definition | Use | Safety / Language Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| concept accuracy | Correct use and explanation of psychology terms. | Rubrics and feedback. | Assess the work, not the student. |
| application | Using a concept to explain a scenario, source, or task. | Scenario tasks and capstones. | Application is not diagnosis. |
| synthesis | Combining more than one idea or domain into a coherent explanation. | Capstones. | Avoid forced complexity without clarity. |
| transfer | Using learning in a new context. | Capstones and reflections. | Evidence of flexible understanding. |
| responsible language | Careful wording that avoids overclaiming, stigma, diagnosis, or unsupported certainty. | Rubrics and feedback. | Required across all cycles. |
| look-for | Specific evidence a teacher uses to evaluate student work. | Marking guides. | Teacher-facing unless converted to student success criteria. |
| success criteria | Student-facing description of what strong work includes. | Assignments and rubrics. | Should be clear and non-shaming. |
| misconception | Common inaccurate or incomplete understanding. | Teacher guides. | Do not frame misconceptions as student flaws. |
| evidence limit | Boundary on what a source, method, or example can prove. | Evidence analysis. | Core assessment habit. |
| safety boundary | Instruction that prevents unsafe disclosure, diagnosis, or role-play. | All high-risk tasks. | Must be explicit in Cycles C and D. |

## 11. Terms to Use Carefully or Avoid in Student-Facing Materials

| Term / Pattern | Use Carefully Because | Safer Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| diagnose | Students are not clinicians. | identify possible concepts; analyze the scenario; describe evidence and limits |
| toxic | Often becomes moral labelling. | harmful pattern; risk; concern; behaviour with possible impact |
| normal / abnormal | Can stigmatize. | common / uncommon; typical / atypical; distressing; impairing |
| crazy / insane | Stigmatizing and imprecise. | inaccurate, distressed, risky, unusual, unsupported |
| manipulative | Can become personal attack. | influence strategy; pressure; coercive pattern; persuasive design |
| trauma response | Can invite disclosure and overdiagnosis. | stress response; threat response; coping pattern, when source-supported |
| addicted | Often overused for habits and technology. | habit loop; repeated use; problematic use, when evidence supports it |
| hardwired | Overstates biology. | influenced by brain/body systems; associated with biological processes |
| proven | Often too strong. | supported by evidence; suggests; is associated with |
| always / never | Usually overclaims. | often; may; can; in this context; evidence suggests |

## 12. Build Notes for Future Student Glossaries

Future student-facing glossaries should:

- use short, clear definitions
- include one low-risk example per term
- include one non-example where useful
- flag terms that students commonly overuse
- separate vocabulary checks from answer keys
- align vocabulary to the active cycle's capstone
- include responsible-language reminders

Teacher-facing vocabulary supports may include:

- misconception notes
- acceptable answer ranges
- common overclaims
- pronunciation support where useful
- links to source chapters or sections once source-register work is complete

## 13. Immediate Next Task

Use this vocabulary bank to begin Cycle A assessment planning. Cycle A should be the first cycle to receive a normalized student assessment pack and teacher marking guide because it teaches the evidence, claim, ethics, and limitation language used by every later cycle.
