# Psychology 11/12 A-F Lens Matrix

## 1. Purpose

This matrix turns the lens-based Psychology 11/12 architecture into a usable planning control panel. It maps the major domains of psychology across six semester lenses so that every cycle can touch the breadth of psychology while still feeling coherent, distinct, and purposeful.

The course should not become a random topic grab bag. It should operate as a structured menu: each cycle has a dominant lens, and each lens selects psychology topics in a way that serves its core question.

## 2. Operating Rule

Every cycle has one dominant lens. Every cycle touches the major domains of psychology. The depth changes by lens.

Every unit should check four permanent through-lines:

1. Cultural / contextual: How do culture, community, identity, language, power, family, environment, and access shape this?
2. Developmental / lifespan: How does this change across childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and older adulthood?
3. Biological / brain-body: What brain, body, nervous system, endocrine, hormonal, sleep, stress, or biological systems are involved, and what are the limits of that explanation?
4. Ethical / evidence: How do we know? What are the limits? What responsibilities come with this knowledge?

## 3. Cycle Lenses

| Cycle | Lens | Core Question | Working Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Scientific / Evidence | How do psychologists know what they claim to know? | Builds disciplinary habits: evidence, methods, ethics, limits, and claim analysis. |
| B | Intellectual / Learning | How do humans learn, think, create, and adapt? | Explores learning, memory, neuroplasticity, cognition, creativity, education, and skill development. |
| C | Relational / Identity | How are humans shaped by connection, belonging, sexuality, personality, and social context? | Explores the social brain, relational memory, identity, relationships, sexuality, and belonging. |
| D | Wellness / Change | How do people cope, struggle, change, and access support? | Explores stress, coping, mental health literacy, treatment, resilience, and behaviour change. |
| E | Justice / Responsibility | How should psychology be used when harm, safety, memory, law, and accountability are involved? | Explores memory, bias, responsibility, forensic psychology, legal systems, risk, and ethics. |
| F | Systems / Media / Influence | How are humans shaped by designed environments, media, persuasion, and technology? | Explores attention, habits, persuasion, media, consumer psychology, AI, and ethical design. |

## 5. Cycle A Module Menu: Scientific / Evidence Lens

Core question: How do psychologists know what they claim to know?

Primary role: Cycle A builds the discipline. It teaches students to separate observation, inference, claim, evidence, opinion, correlation, causation, and ethical limits.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Psychology as science | What is psychology? Psychology vs advice/pop psychology/common sense. | behaviour, mental processes, science, claim, evidence | Misconception sort; claim/evidence sorting task. | Avoid turning psychology into self-help advice. |
| Research methods | Descriptive, correlational, experimental research; variables and operational definitions. | hypothesis, variable, sample, bias, experiment, correlation | Design a mini research proposal using neutral topics. | Do not ask students to research classmates' private lives. |
| Brain/body | Intro to brain-behaviour claims and limits. | nervous system, brain, behaviour, biological explanation | Evaluate a simplified brain claim for overclaiming. | Avoid brain-based determinism. |
| Cognition | Bias, inference, overconfidence, causation errors. | inference, bias, causation, third variable | Correlation vs causation scenarios. | Keep examples neutral and non-shaming. |
| Ethics | Consent, privacy, risk, benefit, deception, debriefing. | informed consent, confidentiality, debriefing, risk | Ethics board scenario review. | Use fictional studies. |
| Applied psychology | Evaluating psychological claims in media and school contexts. | source, credibility, evidence quality, limitation | Media claim audit. | Avoid diagnosing public figures. |

Capstone: Evaluate a psychological claim. Students identify the claim, evidence, limits, possible correlation/causation issues, ethical concerns, and a careful conclusion.

## 6. Cycle B Module Menu: Intellectual / Learning Lens

Core question: How do humans learn, think, create, and adapt?

Primary role: Cycle B studies learning, memory, cognition, creativity, intelligence, motivation, education, and applied skill development.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brain/body | Neuroplasticity, attention, sleep, movement, stress and learning. | neuroplasticity, attention, arousal, sleep, brain network | Explain how a fictional learner improves a skill. | Avoid implying students are fixed by brain differences. |
| Learning | Classical conditioning, operant conditioning, reinforcement, practice, feedback. | reinforcement, punishment, conditioning, feedback | Build a practice plan for a fictional skill. | Avoid manipulative behaviour-control framing. |
| Memory | Encoding, storage, retrieval, forgetting, retrieval practice. | encoding, retrieval, working memory, long-term memory | Compare weak vs strong study strategies. | Avoid public ranking of student ability. |
| Cognition | Problem-solving, bias, creativity, intelligence. | heuristic, bias, intelligence, creativity, divergent thinking | Creativity/problem-solving challenge with reflection. | Treat intelligence testing critically and carefully. |
| Development | Executive function, motivation, adolescence, learning over time. | development, executive function, motivation | Case study: adolescent learner and support design. | Avoid deficit framing. |
| Social/culture | Teacher expectations, peer effects, stereotype threat, language, access. | stereotype threat, expectation, access, equity | Analyze how context affects a fictional student's learning. | Avoid putting students on the spot about identity or class. |
| Applied psychology | Education, sports, workplace training, AI, industry. | transfer, expertise, training, design | Design an evidence-based learning system. | Emphasize agency and ethics. |

Capstone: Design an evidence-based learning or performance support for a fictional person, team, class, or workplace. Students must explain brain/body, learning, memory, motivation, context, evidence, and ethics.

## 7. Cycle C Module Menu: Relational / Identity Lens

Core question: How are humans shaped by connection, belonging, sexuality, personality, and social context?

Primary role: Cycle C studies relationships, identity, sexuality, personality, belonging, social interpretation, and the relational shaping of behaviour.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brain/body | Social brain, reward, threat, bonding, rejection, regulation. | reward, threat, regulation, cortisol, oxytocin | Explain a fictional social stress response. | Avoid reducing relationships to chemicals. |
| Memory | Relational memory, emotional memory, expectations. | emotional memory, schema, expectation, rejection sensitivity | Analyze how past interactions shape a fictional character's interpretation. | Do not ask for students' relationship histories. |
| Cognition | Attribution, assumptions, misinterpretation, unsupported inferences about others' thoughts. | attribution, inference, fundamental attribution error | Relationship misunderstanding scenario. | Use fictional scenarios. |
| Personality/identity | Traits, temperament, self-concept, identity. | trait, temperament, self-concept, identity | Compare personality explanations without labelling people. | Avoid public personality sorting that exposes students. |
| Human sexuality/biology | Puberty, attraction, hormones, sexuality, consent, health literacy. | puberty, hormone, attraction, consent, boundary | Consent and communication case analysis. | Keep academic, respectful, non-disclosure-based. |
| Social/culture | Belonging, norms, prejudice, gender norms, family/community. | belonging, norm, culture, prejudice, inclusion | Analyze a fictional school/community belonging scenario. | Avoid forcing personal identity disclosure. |
| Mental health | Loneliness, rejection, support, relational stress. | social support, loneliness, stress, resilience | Social support map for a fictional character. | Do not ask students to map their own private supports publicly. |

Capstone: Analyze a fictional relationship, media scene, or public scenario using brain/body, memory, cognition, personality, culture, development, ethics, and evidence. The goal is careful explanation without diagnosis or overclaiming.

## 8. Cycle D Module Menu: Wellness / Change Lens

Core question: How do people cope, struggle, change, and access support?

Primary role: Cycle D studies stress, health, coping, mental health literacy, treatment, resilience, support systems, and responsible help-seeking.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brain/body | Stress response, nervous system, endocrine system, sleep, health. | stressor, sympathetic nervous system, cortisol, sleep | Explain stress response in a fictional scenario. | Avoid trauma disclosure. |
| Learning | Habits, avoidance, reinforcement, coping patterns. | avoidance, reinforcement, habit, coping | Behaviour-change plan for a fictional low-risk habit. | Do not ask students to disclose personal struggles. |
| Memory/cognition | Appraisal, rumination, thought patterns, problem-solving. | appraisal, rumination, coping thought, bias | Compare helpful vs unhelpful coping thoughts in scenarios. | Keep examples mild and fictional. |
| Emotion/motivation | Emotion regulation, resilience, needs, persistence. | regulation, resilience, motivation, support | Emotion regulation strategy analysis. | Do not make it therapy practice. |
| Development/culture | Risk/protective factors, stigma, access, help-seeking. | risk factor, protective factor, stigma, access | Analyze how culture and access affect support. | Avoid asking students to name family mental health experiences. |
| Mental health literacy | Distress vs disorder, diagnosis boundaries, myths, stigma. | distress, disorder, impairment, diagnosis, stigma | Myth-busting source analysis. | No self-diagnosis or peer diagnosis. |
| Treatment/support | Therapy approaches, medication literacy, school/community supports, referral pathways. | therapy, referral, support system, confidentiality | Support-awareness resource. | No crisis role-play or peer counselling. |

Capstone: Create a mental health literacy or support-awareness product, or analyze a fictional case with clear boundaries: concept accuracy, stigma reduction, support literacy, and appropriate referral awareness.

## 9. Cycle E Module Menu: Justice / Responsibility Lens

Core question: How should psychology be used when harm, safety, memory, law, and accountability are involved?

Primary role: Cycle E studies legal and forensic applications of psychology with strong attention to evidence, memory reliability, bias, risk, responsibility, and ethics.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brain/body | Threat, arousal, impulse control, trauma-informed caution. | arousal, threat, impulse control, regulation | Analyze decision-making under stress in a sanitized case. | Avoid graphic harm details. |
| Memory | Eyewitness testimony, false memory, confidence, misinformation. | eyewitness testimony, false memory, confidence, misinformation effect | Eyewitness reliability case analysis. | Do not use traumatic graphic cases. |
| Cognition | Bias, attribution, stereotyping, moral reasoning, judgment. | bias, attribution, stereotype, moral reasoning | Legal judgment bias scenarios. | Avoid inflammatory real-world debates unless carefully framed. |
| Social/culture | Authority, obedience, conformity, jury dynamics, policing bias. | obedience, conformity, authority, group decision-making | Structured jury-decision analysis using a fictional low-risk case. | Use sanitized scenarios. |
| Development | Adolescence, risk-taking, responsibility, rehabilitation. | adolescent development, risk, responsibility, rehabilitation | Case study on adolescent responsibility. | Avoid deterministic language about youth. |
| Mental health/law | Competency, responsibility, stigma, treatment vs punishment. | competency, responsibility, stigma, assessment | Evaluate misconceptions about mental health and law. | No diagnosis of real people. |
| Ethics | Expert testimony, confidentiality limits, assessment ethics, justice. | expert testimony, confidentiality, forensic assessment | Forensic ethics board review. | No true-crime entertainment framing. |

Capstone: Analyze a sanitized fictional legal/forensic case file. Students explain what psychology can and cannot responsibly conclude using memory, cognition, social influence, development, evidence, and ethics.

## 10. Cycle F Module Menu: Systems / Media / Influence Lens

Core question: How are humans shaped by designed environments, media, persuasion, and technology?

Primary role: Cycle F studies attention, behaviour design, media systems, persuasion, misinformation, social media, consumer psychology, AI, and ethical design.

| Domain | Possible Modules | Key Vocabulary | Possible Assessment / Activity | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brain/body | Reward, novelty, attention, habit loops, arousal. | reward, novelty, attention, cue, habit loop | Analyze a notification or app design. | Avoid shaming students for technology use. |
| Learning | Reinforcement, cues, rewards, friction, behaviour design. | reinforcement, cue, reward, friction, behaviour design | Redesign a system to support autonomy. | Avoid manipulative design as student objective. |
| Memory/cognition | Misinformation, fluency, source confusion, cognitive load. | misinformation, fluency, cognitive load, source confusion | Media misinformation audit. | Use neutral or fictional examples when a real-world topic would derail the psychology focus. |
| Social/identity | Social comparison, parasocial relationships, influencer culture, norms. | social comparison, parasocial relationship, norm, identity signalling | Analyze social media identity pressures through a fictional example. | Avoid public disclosure of students' online habits. |
| Consumer psychology | Scarcity, branding, emotion, motivation, status. | scarcity, branding, status, motivation | Advertising analysis. | Avoid body-image shaming and manipulative targeting. |
| Ethics | Dark patterns, consent, autonomy, youth audiences, AI. | dark pattern, autonomy, consent, algorithm, persuasion | Ethical design review. | Teach agency and design awareness, not moral panic. |
| Applied psychology | UX, advertising, gaming, social media, AI, public messaging. | user experience, persuasive design, algorithm, media literacy | Audit a media, app, ad, or technology system. | Emphasize evidence and limits. |

Capstone: Conduct a media, technology, advertising, gaming, or consumer-system audit. Students identify psychological mechanisms, evidence, ethical concerns, cultural/contextual factors, and design recommendations.

1. Convert this matrix into `course_map.md`.

4. Build Cycle A first because it is the clearest active source area.
5. Then build cycle overviews before creating detailed lesson packages.

## 13. Matrix QA Checklist

Use this checklist before turning a cycle into a semester plan.

- Does the cycle have one dominant lens?
- Does the cycle still touch the major psychology domains?
- Are culture/context, development, brain-body, and ethics/evidence visible?
- Is there a clear capstone that synthesizes the lens?
- Are student-facing tasks safe and non-disclosure-based?
- Are teacher-facing supports separated from student-facing materials?
- Are OpenStax concepts used as the academic spine without reproducing textbook material directly?
- Are Cycles E and F treated as mapped/future cycles until active files exist?
